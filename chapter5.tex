\chapter{Synthesis and Cross-Paper Discussion}
\label{chap:ch5}

This chapter brings together the findings from Chapters~3 and~4 and discusses what they
mean when considered jointly. The word \textit{synthesis} here simply means combining the results from two separate
studies into a single, broader picture. The two studies address different aspects of the same
problem, operational zone classification for wind turbine SCADA data, and the results
from one study directly inform the interpretation of the other.
Figure~\ref{fig:ch5:lifecycle} shows how both studies could connect to the full PHM digital
twin lifecycle of a real offshore wind turbine, from raw SCADA data through zone
classification and ML-based predictions to condition-based maintenance decisions.

\section{From Zone Definition to Zone Classification}
\label{sec:ch5:foundation}

The two studies in this thesis are not independent. The zone framework developed in
Chapter~3 is the foundation on which everything in Chapter~4 is built. The zone labels
that RIPPER and FOIL are trained to produce are the ground truth labels that all models
in Chapter~4 are trained and evaluated on. The RIPPER one-vs-rest probability outputs
become input features for the DRL agents. The quality of the Chapter~3 framework
directly determines the quality of every result in Chapter~4.

This dependency is also a strength of the thesis structure. By starting with a
physics-informed zone definition and a careful labelling procedure, the thesis avoids the
common problem of machine learning studies that evaluate a classifier on labels that are
themselves uncertain or arbitrary. The labels used in this thesis can be verified against
known turbine physics, and the 100\% accuracy of the raw-SCADA Random Forest in
Chapter~4 confirms that the labels are consistent with the physical reality of the
turbine's behaviour.

\section{What the 100\% Accuracy Result Means for the Zone Framework}
\label{sec:ch5:rf100}

The finding that a Random Forest achieves 100\% accuracy using only four raw SCADA
signals can be interpreted in two ways, both of which are correct and both of which are
important.

The first interpretation is about the zone framework. 100\% accuracy means that the
eight zones present in the dataset can be separated perfectly in the
four-dimensional SCADA space. There is no overlap between zones, no point in the sensor space where it is
unclear which zone an observation belongs to. This is strong evidence that
the physics-informed zone definitions in Chapter~3 are well-specified. The zones capture
genuinely different physical states rather than arbitrary partitions of the data.

The second interpretation is about the rule-based classifiers. If the zones are perfectly
separable with four continuous features, then RIPPER and FOIL's 91--93\% accuracy is not
a reflection of hard zones but of the discretization constraint. The rule-based methods
were never going to achieve 100\% with binned features, because the bins place a hard
ceiling on accuracy. Knowing this changes how we should interpret the Chapter~3 results:
they are close to the ceiling for their method, not far from the ceiling for the problem.

Together, these two interpretations suggest a practical strategy for a wind turbine
digital twin: use rule-based models (RIPPER) to make the zone definitions interpretable
and verifiable, and use a supervised classifier (Random Forest) for actual zone
identification in production. The rule-based model explains what each zone means in
physical terms. The Random Forest identifies which zone each observation belongs to with
100\% accuracy on both evaluation sets.

\section{Interpretability, Accuracy, and Deployability: A Three-Way Trade-Off}
\label{sec:ch5:tradeoff}

The results across both chapters reveal a clear three-way trade-off between
interpretability, accuracy, and deployability. Each approach in this thesis sits at a
different point in this trade-off space.

RIPPER and FOIL are the most interpretable: their rules can be read, verified, and
explained to a non-expert. They are the least accurate (91--93\%) due to discretization
constraints. They are relatively easy to deploy, running fast and requiring no special
infrastructure, but they cannot adapt to changes in turbine behaviour without being
retrained from new labelled data.

The Random Forest and Gradient Boosting classifiers are the most accurate (99.9--100\%)
but the least interpretable: they produce predictions without a human-readable
explanation. Feature importance scores give some indication of which variables drive the
predictions, but not the specific decision logic. They are straightforward to deploy and
run very fast, but like the rule-based models, they are static once trained.

The DRL agents, particularly DQN, occupy a middle position. Accuracy is lower than
supervised classifiers (97.7\% for DQN vs.\ 100\% for RF) but higher than rule-based
methods. They are not more interpretable than supervised classifiers in terms of their internal logic:
the policy network is a black box. However, because the DRL agents in this thesis
receive RIPPER probabilities as part of what they observe, the zone boundaries that
RIPPER captures are implicitly present in the agent's inputs. The physics guardrails
add a further visible layer by making the two clearest physical boundaries explicit
and enforced. Their main advantage is deployability in settings where labelled data
is unavailable or where the model needs to update during operation.

\section{The Role of Physics in Machine Learning for PHM}
\label{sec:ch5:physics}

A recurring theme across both studies is the role of physical knowledge in making machine
learning models more reliable. In Chapter~3, physics knowledge is used to define the zone
boundaries: the thresholds on blade pitch, wind speed, and rotor RPM that separate one
operating state from another. These boundaries are not learned from data; they are derived
from the known control logic of the turbine. This means the zone definitions are valid
from the first day of operation, before any data has been collected.

In Chapter~4, physics knowledge is embedded in the DRL reward function through the
zone-distance penalty, which assumes a physically meaningful ordering of the zones, and
in the guardrail rules, which directly enforce the two clearest physical constraints.
This physics-informed reward design guides the DRL agent away from physically impossible
predictions even during training, which speeds up learning and improves the quality of
the learned policy.

The general lesson is that physics knowledge and data-driven models are not in
competition. They are complementary. Physics knowledge reduces the search space for
data-driven models and provides interpretable constraints that make the models more
trustworthy. Data-driven models can identify patterns that are too complex to specify
manually. The best results in this thesis come from combining both: physics-informed zone
definitions (Chapter~3) with data-driven classifiers trained on physics-consistent labels
(Chapter~4). This principle extends across wind energy research. \citet{gijon2024} show
that a hybrid physics-plus-ML model for wind turbine power prediction achieves 37\%
higher accuracy than a physics model alone while remaining interpretable through
explainability analysis. Similarly, \citet{hong2025} demonstrate that embedding physics
constraints directly into the training process of a SCADA anomaly detector produces more
reliable and interpretable results than purely data-driven outlier removal.

\section{Practical Recommendations for Wind Farm Operators}
\label{sec:ch5:recommendations}

Based on the results of both studies, the following practical recommendations can be made
for wind farm operators and digital twin developers:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{For production zone classification where labelled training data is
        available:} use a Random Forest or Gradient Boosting classifier trained on the
        four raw SCADA signals. This achieves 100\% accuracy, runs fast, and requires no
        special infrastructure.

  \item \textbf{For understanding and verifying the zone definitions:} use the RIPPER
        rule set. The rules make explicit which physical conditions define each zone and
        can be checked by a domain expert without any machine learning knowledge.

  \item \textbf{For deployment on a new turbine type or where labelled data is not
        available:} DQN with physics guardrails is the recommended starting point. It
        reaches 97.7\% accuracy in this study and can, in principle, be updated online
        using reward signals computed from physics rules.

  \item \textbf{For reducing the number of readings that need human review:} use
        Pipeline~B, which places DQN before a LightGBM meta-model. This reduces flagged
        readings from 8.0\% to 2.3\% of the dataset while maintaining 98.9\% recall of
        the remaining errors and producing only 14 false alarms across 52,234 test
        readings.

  \item \textbf{Avoid A2C for this task.} Its failure on Zone~8 (full load operation) is
        a significant problem for any application that depends on correct identification
        of the turbine's highest-power operating state.

  \item \textbf{Apply physics guardrails to any DRL agent used for zone classification.}
        They cost nothing for a well-trained agent (DQN corrects only 0.05\% of
        predictions) and provide a meaningful safety net for weaker agents.
\end{itemize}
